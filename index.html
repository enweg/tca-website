Overview

Impulse response functions only measure the total effect of a shock or policy. What if we wanted to know how this total effect comes to be, i.e. what if we wanted to understand how the shock or policy work? Transmission Channel Analysis allows for the decomposition of impulse response functions into the effects along channels. Each channel corresponds to one mechanism behind the shock or the policy.

Example

Wegner et al (2024) use Transmission Channel Analysis (TCA) to decompose the impulse response functions of a contractionary monetary policy shock. They are able to quantify the wage channel within a theoretical model and show that wages play a key role in the transmission of monetary policy.

Since TCA decomposes the impulse response function, Wegner et al (2024) are able to produce a decomposition plot, showing their results graphically.

Software

We provide various software packages for TCA. These allow practitioners to apply TCA to a broad range of problems. Currently, TCA packages exist in Julia, Matlab, and for Dynare (as part of the Matlab package). See the documentation or the tutorials for each of them to learn more

What is the Dynamic Form?

TCA makes use of two forms (1) the dynamic form and (2) the systems form. In this article we describe what the dynamic form is.
What is the Systems Form?

TCA makes use of two forms (1) the dynamic form and (2) the systems form. In this article we describe what the systems form is.
What is the Associated Graph?

Transmission channels are best thought of as paths through the graph associted with the model. In this article we describe was the associated graph is.
What are Boolean Conditions?

Transmission conditions are most effectively stated using Boolean conditions about which variables should and should not lie on the transmission channels.

How do I cite TCA?

If you use TCA or any of the packages in your research, we would appreciate if you could cite the original paper. This is currently the best way.
